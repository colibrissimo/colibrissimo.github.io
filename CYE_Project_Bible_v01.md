# 1. What CYE Is

## 1.1 The Core Idea

CYE is a pedagogical system that teaches English grammar through synaesthetic principles â€” colour, texture, sound, and spatial relationships. Students don't learn rules and then apply them. They experience grammatical categories as sensory properties, building intuitive pattern recognition before (or alongside) explicit knowledge.

This isn't decoration. It's grounded in Cognitive Load Theory: multi-sensory input opens parallel processing channels, reducing the load on any single channel. The colour IS the grammar. The texture IS the function. There is no legend to cross-reference, no key to look up.

## 1.2 The Three Rings

**Ring 1 â€” Core:** A teaching tool. Interactive activities using colour, texture, and sound to teach specific grammar points (phrasal verbs, tenses, adjective order, etc.) to L2 English learners. This is the prototype. This is what gets built first.

**Ring 2 â€” Framework:** A design methodology for multi-sensory learning. The Sensory Vault, the Climate Zones, the Warp/Weft metaphor. Applicable beyond English, beyond grammar, beyond language teaching. This is the system.

**Ring 3 â€” Horizon:** A civilisational hypothesis. If language shapes perception (Sapir-Whorf), then raising children with dual-mode symbolic language â€” sequential AND simultaneous, spoken AND patterned â€” could fundamentally reshape human-AI communication. This is the long game. It connects to the Bridge Protocol work. It is real but distant.

*Working principle: Build Ring 1 first. Let Ring 2 emerge from the building. Let Ring 3 remain a guiding star, not a distraction.*

## 1.3 The Name Problem

"Colour Your English" is taken (Hirschman & Bautz, Perceptia Press). A new name is needed. The name should convey sensing language, not just colouring it. Candidates explored and shelved: "Pigment Your English" (too close), "English Loom" (beautiful but loses the sensory core), "EnglishScape" (no). The answer hasn't arrived yet. It will come when Sarah's not looking.

# 2. Design Decisions Made

## 2.1 The Sensory Vault

The Sensory Vault is NOT a universal colour-grammar dictionary. It is a set of design principles for assigning sensory mappings within any given activity.

**Constraint:** Lindsey & Brown (2014) established a perceptual ceiling of ~15â€“16 reliable colour categories in English speakers. CYE activities use 3â€“4 colour mappings maximum per activity. Exceeding this hits the perceptual ceiling and creates confusion, not learning.

**Principle:** Warm colours for semantic content (nouns, verbs, adjectives â€” things that carry meaning). Cool colours for structural/functional elements (prepositions, conjunctions, articles â€” things that organise meaning). Textures for weight/temporality (rough/heavy for past, smooth/light for future). Sound for movement/direction (rising for questions, falling for statements).

**Key insight:** Colour mappings are activity-specific, not universal. The grammar mindmap (Basic English Grammar) has hundreds of potential categories. Each activity picks a small meal from the menu. The mindmap is the menu, not the plate.

## 2.2 Climate Zones (Expertise Progression)

CYE uses tiered zones that scaffold differently based on learner expertise. This is grounded in Kalyuga's Expertise Reversal Effect: instructional designs that help novices can actively hinder advanced learners.

**Lollipop Playground:** Heavy colour-coding, detailed guidance, full sensory scaffolding. For A1â€“A2 learners.

**The Meadow:** Reduced scaffolding. Student becomes "Gardener" â€” chooses how to "plant" knowledge. The Third Space where fixed grammar rules (Warp) meet student choice (Weft). For B1â€“B2 learners.

**The Architecture:** Minimal colour-coding. Scaffolds faded. Student has built internal schemas; redundant support wastes cognitive resources. For C1+ learners.

## 2.3 The Third Space

**The Warp (Space 2):** Fixed grammar rules. Non-negotiable.

**The Weft (Space 1):** Student's choice. Their vocabulary, their hue, their personal connection to the language.

The student weaves Warp and Weft together, creating something that is both structurally correct and personally meaningful.

## 2.4 Dyslexia and Accessibility

CYE's design inherently benefits dyslexic learners, but this must be intentional, not accidental. Visual attention span, pattern-recognition over text-search, dyslexia-friendly fonts, teal-orange schemes, avoidance of pure white backgrounds.

## 2.5 Teal and Orange

Teal is one of four emerging colour terms in American English (Lindsey & Brown, 2014). It sits at the green-blue boundary â€” a liminal, transitional colour. Orange is warm, active, alive. Together they represent the two poles of CYE: cool structure and warm content, architecture and flight.
